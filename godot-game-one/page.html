On a bulky computer in an alternate 1960s, unlock and investigate 3D objects for clues to the death of your clients. Leave no stone unturned in this story-rich supernatural mystery.

GAMEPLAY

The player reads the report, then searches for relevant objects by typing. The player can view each object in 3D, and move it around to find hidden details and unlock new names. Collect more objects, details, and names to piece together the true cause of death.

3D models in the 1960s? It's not so far-fetched in this story, where the development of the camera was stunted, and the personal computer catapulted forward by an emerging science, Lexicontology.

Ontological capture devices (like the Vit-Alert inside every Death Insurance client) can scan and store a Snapshot of the essence of every object and person in a small area.

Their Lexicontological counterparts (like the TODO machine on your desk) can filter a Snapshot using text input, and return a single Object if the resonance between Object and text is close enough. An Ontological Object contains everything that makes say, an apple an apple. So displaying its shape, texture, and shade on a monitor is as easy as apple pie.

Power consumption, processor speeds, and display technologies are bottlenecks today, but with enough resources, the possibilities of Lexicontology are endless.

Each case in Death Insurance has an information pack with everything the player needs to prove the client's true cause of death.

  • The transcript of a voice memo from your Retriever. They are called to the 'scene of the crime' and are trained to describe the situation and report any notable objects.
  • A 'courtroom sketch' style drawing of the scene from your Retriever.
  • The memory card of the deceased client's Vit-Alert device, containing an Ontological Snapshot of the scene at the moment of death.
    • Dock the memory card and print out the logs to obtain metadata like the time of death / when the Snapshot was taken, and when the Retriever arrived on the scene.

There are loads of hand-crafted 3D models to find during the investigation, each with at least one detail to catalogue. To unlock these Objects, the player types into the search box. As the query gets closer to a valid object the swirl will fade and the object will become clearer. There's also an extensive list of synonyms and common misspellings for each object. Unlocked objects, and their respective details are stored in an object list for easy recall. Objects with all details found are marked as complete in the object list.

While viewing an unlocked object, the player can rotate it in 3D, and pan around. When the cursor at the centre of the screen overlaps with a detail hitbox, the detail will be unlocked and its text displayed in an overlay. Hovering over a detail from the object list will show the text in an overlay for quick reference. Some detail text will contain names that are automatically added to the name list. Some details will lead the player to search for new objects not mentioned in the report.

When the player has collected enough objects/names and formed a hypothesis, they can open the FE6 form. Select the perpetrator's first and last name, and select an object as the instrument of death. The form is validated immediately, and the case is completed when all fields are correct.

STORY

A job's a job right? This new one's properly weird though. You're another cog in the machine, investigating life insurance claims for recently deceased clients. Each case is sent to you by a Retriever in the field. Based on the details of this report, you type in possible objects and examine different angles of these for clues to the cause of death. As you populate your list of objects, strange anomalies become apparent (a letter opener that looks like a ritual knife, a paperweight in the shape of a disturbing entity). Curiosity leads you further and further into you own secret side investigation into a supernatural conspiracy of cosmic proportions.
